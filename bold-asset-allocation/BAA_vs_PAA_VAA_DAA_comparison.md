# BAA vs. PAA, VAA, and DAA: A Comparison

*Based on Keller & Keuning's PAA (2016), VAA (2017), DAA (2018), and Keller's Bold Asset Allocation (BAA, 2022)*

## 1. How BAA differs from its predecessors

BAA is explicitly built as a hybrid that cherry-picks pieces from all three earlier models, then adds one new idea (a richer defensive universe). The paper is unusually transparent about this recipe:

| Feature | PAA (2016) | VAA (2017) | DAA (2018) | **BAA (2022)** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Relative-momentum filter (asset selection) | Slow SMA(12) | Fast 13612W | Fast 13612W | **Slow SMA(12)** (borrowed from PAA) — used for *both* offensive and defensive selection |
| Absolute/crash-protection filter | Same SMA(12), applied to the invested universe itself | Same fast 13612W, applied to the invested universe itself | Fast 13612W, applied to a separate **canary** universe | **Fast 13612W**, applied to a separate 4-asset canary universe (borrowed from DAA's canary concept, using VAA's own G4 universe as the canary) |
| Breadth parameter (B) | Effectively N/4, N/2 depending on protection level a | N/A (winner-take-all, no breadth blend) | Typically a mid-sized breadth threshold on a small canary universe | **B=1** — the most aggressive possible setting: a *single* bad canary asset flips the entire portfolio to defensive |
| Defensive universe | Cash-like: 1-2 assets (IEF, or IEF/SHY combo) | A handful of bond/cash alternatives, single best selected | Small defensive set, single best selected | **Enlarged to 7 assets** (TIP, DBC, BIL, IEF, TLT, LQD, BND) — commodities (DBC) and inflation-linked TIP added explicitly as an inflation/rising-rate hedge, with **Top3** selected using both relative *and* absolute momentum |
| Offensive universe | 12-asset global "risky" universe, Top6 equal-weight | Aggressive: 4-asset global universe, Top1 (winner-take-all) | Similar to VAA, small universe | Same choice as PAA **or** VAA depending on variant: BAA-G12 (Top6, like PAA) or BAA-G4 (Top1, like VAA, with QQQ swapped in for SPY) |
| Time spent defensive | ~50% (DF≈50.7%) | Not directly reported, but generally high given fast filter | Lower than VAA (canary reduces false positives from the full universe) | **~57–60% (DF≈57–59.5%)** — *higher* than PAA despite DAA's canary concept being designed to reduce time in cash |
| Turnover | 284%/year | Very high (fast filter + winner-take-all) | High, but lower than VAA | **472–523%/year** — nearly double PAA's, driven by both the fast canary filter and the added Top3 defensive rotation |
| New performance metric | Standard R, V, D, SR, MAR | Standard | Standard | **Introduces "K" (Keller Ratio)** — a proprietary return/risk measure that zeroes out entirely once drawdown exceeds 25% |
| In-sample split | Single IS (1970–92) / OS (1992–2015) split | Similar single-split approach | Similar | **Two-part IS** (IS1: 1970–80 rising-rate decade; IS2: 2012–22 low-rate decade) averaged into "K/IS", explicitly designed to reward strategies that do well in *both* regimes |

**In one sentence:** BAA takes PAA's slow relative-momentum stock-picking, bolts on DAA's separate canary-universe concept but dials its trigger to maximum sensitivity (B=1), borrows VAA's aggressive concentrated-offensive-universe option, and then compensates for the resulting heavy time-in-cash by turning the "cash" sleeve into a diversified, commodity-enhanced multi-asset bond portfolio.

## 2. Drawbacks of BAA relative to PAA/VAA/DAA

- **Even higher turnover and trading costs.** At 472–523%/year, BAA trades noticeably more than PAA (284%/year), which directly eats into net-of-cost returns — the paper still assumes a flat 0.1% one-way cost, so this drag is likely understated in the reported figures.
- **DAA's canary concept was meant to *reduce* time spent defensive versus PAA/VAA (by not tying the protection signal to the whole investable universe). BAA reverses that benefit.** With B=1 on only a 4-asset canary, a single asset having a bad month is enough to move the entire portfolio to defense — so BAA actually spends *more* time defensive (57–60%) than PAA (~50%), the opposite of what the canary innovation was designed to achieve.
- **More moving parts, more places to overfit.** BAA has three separate universes (offensive, defensive, protective/canary), two different momentum filters (SMA(12) and 13612W), and both relative and absolute momentum rules within the defensive sleeve — a materially more complex specification than any single one of PAA, VAA, or DAA.
- **A new, non-standard performance metric (K) is introduced alongside — not instead of — the standard measures.** Reporting a bespoke ratio that the same author designed makes it easier, deliberately or not, to favor whichever variant scores best on a metric no independent party has vetted.
- **Concentration risk in the aggressive variant.** BAA-G4 (Top1 offensive selection from only 4 assets) is a much more concentrated bet than PAA's Top6-of-12 or even VAA's Top1-of-4 — a single-asset allocation swings the whole "offensive" sleeve, so idiosyncratic risk in one ETF (e.g., QQQ) has an outsized effect on results.

## 3. Obvious faults / weaknesses in the BAA approach itself

1. **Explicit, acknowledged in-sample tuning of individual design choices.** The paper's own footnotes state plainly that specific choices — swapping SPY for QQQ in the offensive universe (note 10), adding DBC to the defensive universe (note 8), and the SMA(12)/13612W filter combination (note 6) — were each selected because they were "in-sample optimal on K/IS." This is a textbook data-snooping pattern: several discrete design decisions, each individually tuned on the same in-sample criterion used to declare the model a success.
2. **The robustness check for BAA-G4 actually reveals fragility, not robustness.** Fig 14 in the paper shows K/FS collapsing to a flat **0.0%** across an entire block of momentum-filter combinations (SMA(9), SMA(12), and RET(12) protective filters, all offensive filters) — a cliff-edge failure, not a smooth degradation. The paper's narrative ("robustness... provide some trust") glosses over the fact that a large swath of nearby, entirely plausible parameter choices produces a *complete breakdown* of the aggressive variant, which is the opposite of robustness.
3. **B=1 makes the "smooth breadth" mechanism effectively binary again.** PAA's breadth indicator was designed to blend gradually between 0–100% bonds. With a 4-asset canary and B=1, the defensive switch triggers to 100% the instant a single asset (out of just four) turns negative — reintroducing the discrete, all-or-nothing threshold behavior that breadth-momentum was originally meant to smooth away, now with even less diversification behind the trigger (1-of-4 vs. PAA's 6-of-12).
4. **The enhanced defensive universe is itself a regime bet.** Adding commodities (DBC) and TIP as an inflation/rising-rate hedge is calibrated against the 1970s stagflation episode and 2021/22 inflation spike — the two periods the two-part in-sample window was specifically chosen to span (IS1: 1970–80, IS2: 2012–22). A defensive sleeve engineered to do well in exactly the regimes used to select it is a natural candidate for the same kind of in-sample flattery already identified in PAA, now extended to the choice of defensive assets, not just the switching rule.
5. **Two-part in-sample design is itself a modeling choice made with the benefit of hindsight.** Deliberately constructing IS1 (rising rates) and IS2 (low/rising rates plus 2021-22 inflation) and then optimizing the average "K/IS" across exactly those two known-difficult regimes all but guarantees the resulting model will look strong in a 2022-style environment — because that environment was one of only two regimes used to pick the model in the first place.
6. **Same inherited data-construction risk as PAA.** BAA reuses the same monthly ETF-proxy methodology from the original PAA paper's Appendix A, extended through Jun 2022 — so all of PAA's proxy/construction caveats (synthetic pre-inception pricing, bond roll-yield assumptions, tracking-error minimization against a later live ETF) carry over unchanged.
7. **A single flat transaction-cost assumption (0.1%) is used despite turnover nearly doubling versus PAA.** If anything, the case for testing a range of realistic costs is stronger here than for PAA, precisely because BAA trades so much more.
8. **The proprietary "K" metric has a built-in cliff at D=25% (K=0 beyond that), which can obscure differences between strategies whose drawdowns fall on either side of that line** — two strategies with 24% and 40% drawdowns could show similarly low K scores, while a 15% vs. 24% comparison would show a large K gap, even though the economic difference in risk may not track that discontinuity well.
9. **Reported "impressive" results lean on a single, small canary universe (SPY, VWO, VEA, BND) for every BAA variant.** All four BAA models (G12, G4, G12/T3, G4/T2) share the identical canary, so any weakness or historical quirk in that specific 4-asset combination propagates identically into every variant presented — the apparent "robustness across variants" in fig 15 is partly an illusion, since the single most influential design choice (the canary universe and B=1 threshold) was never varied in that comparison.

## Bottom line

BAA is best understood as a "best-of" remix: PAA's stock-selection filter, DAA's canary-universe idea pushed to its most sensitive setting, and a materially richer defensive sleeve added to compensate for spending most of its time out of risk assets. The reported full-sample numbers (R≈21% for the aggressive BAA-G4 variant) are the most eye-catching in the whole model family, but they come with higher turnover, more tuned parameters, and — per the paper's own robustness table — a genuine fragility in the aggressive variant that a large neighborhood of "reasonable" alternative parameter choices completely erases. As with PAA, VAA, and DAA before it, the central open question is how much of BAA's edge is a real, persistent effect versus a historically-fitted response to the exact two in-sample decades (1970s stagflation and the 2010s-into-2022 low-rate/inflation-spike period) chosen to build it.
