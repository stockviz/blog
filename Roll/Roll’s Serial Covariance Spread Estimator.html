Roll’s Serial Covariance Spread Estimator

Exported on: 25/11/2025, 11:36:19
The Roll’s serial covariance spread estimator is an econometric model designed to estimate the average bid/ask spread (or effective spread) of a security using only transaction prices, without needing quotation data. It is one of the best-known estimators based on price change serial covariances.
This model helps analysts measure implicit transaction costs, particularly the average costs for an entire market, and is especially valuable in markets where bid and ask price quotations are not recorded, such as open outcry futures markets.
Core Concepts and Mechanism
1. Price Reversals and Negative Serial Correlation: The model exploits the statistical properties of transaction prices caused by traders demanding liquidity. When traders continuously buy at the ask and sell at the bid (known as bid/ask bounce), the price changes tend to reverse, resulting in negative serial correlation in the sequence of price changes. Since the efficient markets hypothesis suggests that price changes should have zero serial covariance if transaction costs are absent, the presence of negative serial covariance signals transaction costs.
2. Volatility Decomposition: Roll's model is considered one of the simplest statistical models that can estimate the components of total volatility. Total volatility is the sum of fundamental volatility (unpredictable price changes reflecting changes in fundamental values) and transitory volatility (price changes caused by uninformed traders seeking liquidity, which tend to revert).
Model Assumptions and Formula
The model relies on several simplifying assumptions:
• The fundamental value (Vt​) of the security follows a random walk, meaning the change in fundamental value (ϵt​) is independently distributed through time with a zero mean.
• The observed price (Pt​) at time t is equal to the fundamental value (Vt​) plus or minus half of the spread (S), depending on whether the trade was buyer-initiated or seller-initiated.
• The probability that a trader is a buyer (or seller) is one-half and is independent of previous trades or fundamental value changes.
Based on these assumptions, Roll demonstrated that the expected serial covariance of price changes is proportional to the negative square of the spread.
The resulting formula for the Effective Spread (S) is derived by inverting the serial covariance calculation, substituting the sample serial covariance for the expected serial covariance:
S=2–SCov(ΔPt​,ΔPt−1​)​
In this equation, SCov(ΔPt​,ΔPt−1​) represents the expected serial covariance of the price changes.
Limitations
The primary limitation of the Roll’s serial covariance spread estimator is that it predicts that price changes will only be negatively correlated between adjacent transactions. If the price reversal resulting from temporary illiquidity takes longer than a single transaction to occur, the model will underestimate the true transitory volatility component of the price change. More complex models are needed when reversion occurs over multiple intervals.
