Decomposing Volatility: Fundamental and Transitory Components

Exported on: 25/11/2025, 11:37:17
The calculation of fundamental volatility and transitory volatility relies on statistical models that decompose the total price volatility of a security into its permanent and temporary components.
The two components that constitute total volatility (Var(ΔPt​)) are fundamental volatility (σ2), which is associated with changes in fundamental value, and transitory volatility (T), which is associated with temporary illiquidity, such as bid/ask bounce.
Total Volatility=Fundamental Volatility+Transitory Volatility
1. Calculation via Roll’s Serial Covariance Spread Estimator
The Roll’s serial covariance spread estimator model is the simplest statistical model used to estimate and distinguish between these variance components.
This method leverages the distinct statistical properties of the two types of price changes:
• Fundamental volatility consists of seemingly random price changes that do not revert. These changes often have the properties of a random walk.
• Transitory volatility consists of price changes that ultimately revert. This price reversal creates negative serial correlation in the series of price changes.
Under the simplified assumptions of the Roll model (where fundamental value follows a random walk, the price innovation is independent, and the probability of a buyer or seller arriving is half), the total variance of the price change (ΔPt​) is expressed as the sum of the two components:
Var(ΔPt​)=σ2+41​S2
Where:
• σ2 represents the variance due to changes in fundamental values, which is the Fundamental Volatility component.
• 41​S2 represents the variance due to bid/ask bounce, which is the Transitory Volatility component. S is the effective spread.
Steps for Calculation using Roll's Model:
The key to separating these components is estimating the transitory component from the serial covariance of price changes.
1. Calculate the Transitory Volatility Component (T): Roll demonstrated that the expected serial covariance of adjacent price changes (SCov(ΔPt​,ΔPt−1​)) is equal to the negative of the transitory component: SCov(ΔPt​,ΔPt−1​)=−41​S2 Therefore, the Transitory Volatility component is: T=41​S2=−SCov(ΔPt​,ΔPt−1​)
2. In practice, you use the sample serial covariance in place of the expected serial covariance.
3. Calculate the Fundamental Volatility Component (σ2): The fundamental volatility component is derived by subtracting the estimated transitory volatility component (from Step 1) from the calculated total price change variance: σ2=Var(ΔPt​)−41​S2
4. In the context of volatility, price changes due to the adverse selection spread component reflect changes in dealers' estimates of instrument values and contribute to fundamental volatility because they do not systematically reverse. In contrast, the transaction cost spread component (also called the transitory spread component) contributes to transitory volatility via bid/ask bounce.
2. Advanced Econometric Methods
While Roll's model is simple, it predicts that negative serial correlation exists only between adjacent price changes (transaction-to-transaction price changes). If price reversion takes longer than one transaction, the model underestimates transitory volatility.
For more complex analysis, more complex variance component models are used. These sophisticated statistical methods:
• Decompose a price series into a random walk component (Fundamental Volatility) and a mean-reverting component (Transitory Volatility).
• Can account for negative serial correlation that occurs over many intervals, such as minutes, hours, days, or even months, caused by the price impacts of large orders or order imbalances from uninformed traders.
One example of a complex method is the Glosten-Harris spread components estimation model, which uses regression analysis to characterize how prices change in response to order flow. This model separates price effects into:
• A permanent price effect, which corresponds to the adverse selection spread component and is attributed to the fundamental value inference made from order flow.
• A transitory price effect, which corresponds to the transaction cost spread component (costs of supplying liquidity to impatient traders).
These advanced models allow analysts to compute transaction cost estimates for any trade by evaluating expressions for the permanent (fundamental) and transitory price components.

Analogy: Decomposing volatility is like analyzing the movement of a boat on a rough sea. The fundamental volatility is the overall, long-term course of the boat, determined by deep currents and the captain's direction—it represents permanent progress or directional change. The transitory volatility is the chaotic, short-term bobbing and weaving caused by surface waves and wakes from other boats—it's temporary, quickly reversing movement around the underlying course. To calculate the true course (fundamental volatility), you must filter out the temporary surface noise (transitory volatility) from the total observed movement.
